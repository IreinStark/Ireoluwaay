Reflections

Thoughts

Short essays on what certain stories meant. Not recommendations — closer to personal reckonings. Read in the mood the title suggests.

01
Identity Freedom Moral Collapse

Attack on Titan:
The Price of a Dream

On ambition, genocide, and the boy who wanted to see the ocean.

There is a version of Attack on Titan that ends in season one. The walls fall. The boy swears revenge. The titans are monstrous, the heroes pure. It would have been a fine story.

Hajime Isayama chose something else. He chose to make us love a character and then slowly, devastatingly, show us what love built on rage actually produces. Eren Yeager is not a villain by the end. He is the fulfillment of a promise made before he understood what promising meant.

The final arc asks an impossible question: what do we owe the people we love when what they want requires the destruction of everything else? Isayama doesn't answer it cleanly. He sits inside the discomfort. The series ends not in triumph but in silence — and that silence is more honest than almost anything else in the medium.

Death Note:
What Genius Does to a Person

On justice as vanity, and the slow starvation of a brilliant mind.

Light Yagami is not scary because he's evil. He's scary because, for about three episodes, we agree with him. The world is full of people who cause suffering without consequence. If you had the power to stop that — wouldn't you?

Death Note is the long, meticulous answer to that question. What happens is not that Light becomes corrupted — it's that the corruption was always there, and power simply removed the mask. He never cared about justice. He cared about being right. Being superior. Being the one who decided.

By the end, he is alone in a way that no amount of success can remedy. Every person in his life was a chess piece. And chess pieces, it turns out, are very poor company.

Vinland Saga:
The Weight of the Sword

On violence as inheritance, and the long walk toward forgiveness.

Thorfinn does not have a coming-of-age arc. He has a coming-back arc — the long, humbling process of returning to humanity after years of being an instrument of death.

Vinland Saga understands something most shonen don't: strength is not the answer to violence. The answer to violence is the willingness to stop. To lay down the sword not because you can't win, but because you finally understand that winning was never the point.

The second season, largely dismissed for its quietness, is where the series becomes extraordinary. Thorfinn as a slave is more compelling than Thorfinn as a warrior, because for the first time in his life, he has nowhere to run and nothing to prove.

Depression Connection

Evangelion:
The Terror of Being Known

On loneliness as self-preservation, and what it costs to let someone in.

Neon Genesis Evangelion is not a story about giant robots. It is a story about a boy who is so afraid of being rejected that he would rather end the world than risk reaching out again.

Anno made this series during a depressive episode. You feel it in every frame — the long silences, the architectural emptiness, the way characters speak past each other even in intimate moments. Connection is desired and feared simultaneously.

What separates Evangelion from mere wallowing is that it takes its characters' dysfunction seriously. It does not mock Shinji for flinching. It asks: given what was done to him, given who taught him what love looks like — what else was he supposed to learn?

Ambition Ego

Blue Lock:
The Gospel of Ego

On selfishness as a virtue, and the strange morality of excellence.

Blue Lock makes an argument that is uncomfortable to engage with honestly: at the absolute frontier of human performance, selfishness is not a flaw. It is the engine.

Ego Jinpachi's philosophy is disturbing because it works. The boys who embrace their hunger — who stop apologizing for wanting to be the best — are the ones who break through. The ones who defer, who shrink, who play safe, are eliminated.

Is the series endorsing this worldview? I'm not sure. But it renders it compellingly enough that you find yourself rooting for boys who, in any other context, would be insufferable. That is a genuine artistic achievement.
